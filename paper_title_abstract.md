# AAAI Submission — Title & Abstract (Draft)

## Title

**Uncertainty-Guided Error Repair in Multi-Step Language Model Agents: When to Target, When to Restart**

## Abstract

When a multi-step language model agent fails at a complex reasoning task, practitioners face a fundamental recovery question: should the agent redo the single step that went wrong, or restart the entire trajectory from scratch? Targeted repair is cheaper, but it requires knowing *which* step failed — and localizing errors in sequential reasoning chains remains an open problem. We present a systematic study of uncertainty-guided repair strategies for ReAct-style agents across three reasoning benchmarks (HotpotQA, FEVER, and MuSiQue), eight open-weight language models spanning three capacity tiers (7B--72B), and 33 repair strategies. Each failed trajectory is scored at every step using five uncertainty metrics (token entropy, perplexity, max-token probability, self-consistency, and verbalized confidence), and six localization rules — including three novel *cascade-aware* rules — predict the broken step. We then compare four repair paradigms under matched compute budgets: full restart, random-step repair, oracle-targeted repair (upper bound), and uncertainty-targeted repair. Our analysis reveals three findings that challenge conventional assumptions. First, the optimal repair strategy is task-dependent: on multi-hop QA benchmarks with longer reasoning chains — HotpotQA (14.2% full restart vs 10.9% oracle targeted) and MuSiQue (5.9% vs 4.9%) — full restart outperforms even oracle-targeted repair, suggesting that context contamination from earlier erroneous reasoning limits surgical repair. However, on FEVER (fact verification) with shorter trajectories, this pattern reverses: oracle-targeted repair with backtracking (3.2%) edges out full restart (2.8%), indicating that trajectory length is a key moderator of repair strategy effectiveness. Second, backtracking upstream from the identified failure point consistently helps across all tasks (12.4% vs 10.9% on HotpotQA; 3.2% vs 2.5% on FEVER; 5.6% vs 4.9% on MuSiQue), confirming that errors propagate forward through reasoning chains and that cascade-aware localization rules — which account for uncertainty peaking *downstream* of the true failure — improve repair over standard argmax selection. Third, uncertainty-guided strategies consistently outperform random-step repair across all benchmarks and model scales (e.g., 8.1% random vs 10--13% uncertainty-guided on HotpotQA; 1.2% vs 2--3% on FEVER; 3.8% vs 4--6% on MuSiQue), demonstrating that internal model signals carry meaningful information for error localization despite imperfect calibration. Larger models (70--72B) show both higher base success rates and greater repair gains, while smaller models (7--9B) exhibit more diffuse uncertainty that weakens localization. We further analyze the cost-accuracy tradeoff of ensemble strategies that achieve higher fix rates (36.0% on HotpotQA, 9.6% on FEVER, 16.2% on MuSiQue) at substantially greater compute cost. All experiments use controlled comparisons with paired statistical tests, three random seeds, and a validated 72B judge for ground-truth annotation. Code and reproducibility artifacts are publicly available.
