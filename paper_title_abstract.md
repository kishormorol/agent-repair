# AAAI Submission — Title & Abstract (Draft)

## Title

**Uncertainty-Guided Error Repair in Multi-Step Language Model Agents: When to Target, When to Restart**

## Abstract

When a multi-step language model agent fails at a complex reasoning task, practitioners face a fundamental recovery question: should the agent redo the single step that went wrong, or restart the entire trajectory from scratch? Targeted repair is cheaper, but it requires knowing *which* step failed — and localizing errors in sequential reasoning chains remains an open problem. We present a systematic study of uncertainty-guided repair strategies for ReAct-style agents across four multi-hop reasoning benchmarks (HotpotQA, FEVER, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue), eight open-weight language models spanning three capacity tiers (7B--72B), and 33 repair strategies. Each failed trajectory is scored at every step using five uncertainty metrics (token entropy, perplexity, max-token probability, self-consistency, and verbalized confidence), and six localization rules — including three novel *cascade-aware* rules — predict the broken step. We then compare four repair paradigms under matched compute budgets: full restart, random-step repair, oracle-targeted repair (upper bound), and uncertainty-targeted repair. Our analysis reveals three findings that challenge conventional assumptions. First, contrary to expectations, full restart (14.2% fix rate on HotpotQA) outperforms even oracle-targeted repair (10.9%), suggesting that context contamination from earlier erroneous reasoning steps limits the effectiveness of surgical repair — the agent benefits more from a clean slate than from resuming within a corrupted trajectory. Second, backtracking upstream from the identified failure point partially closes this gap (12.4% with two-step backtrack), confirming that errors propagate forward through reasoning chains and that cascade-aware localization rules — which account for uncertainty peaking *downstream* of the true failure — improve repair over standard argmax selection. Third, uncertainty-guided strategies consistently outperform random-step repair (8.1%), demonstrating that internal model signals carry meaningful information for error localization despite imperfect calibration. We further introduce error-informed nudges, where the repair prompt incorporates the predicted error type, and analyze the cost-accuracy tradeoff of ensemble strategies that achieve higher fix rates (36.0%) at substantially greater compute cost. All experiments use controlled comparisons with paired statistical tests, three random seeds, and a validated 72B judge for ground-truth annotation. Code and reproducibility artifacts are publicly available.
