# AAAI Submission — Title & Abstract (Draft)

## Title

**Uncertainty-Guided Error Repair in Multi-Step Language Model Agents: When to Target, When to Restart**

## Abstract

When a multi-step language model agent fails at a complex reasoning task, practitioners face a fundamental recovery question: should the agent redo the single step that went wrong, or restart the entire trajectory from scratch? Targeted repair is cheaper, but it requires knowing *which* step failed — and localizing errors in sequential reasoning chains remains an open problem. We present a systematic study of uncertainty-guided repair strategies for ReAct-style agents across four multi-hop reasoning benchmarks (HotpotQA, FEVER, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue), eight open-weight language models spanning three capacity tiers (7B--72B), and 33 repair strategies. Each failed trajectory is scored at every step using five uncertainty metrics (token entropy, perplexity, max-token probability, self-consistency, and verbalized confidence), and six localization rules — including three novel *cascade-aware* rules — predict the broken step. We then compare four repair paradigms under matched compute budgets: full restart, random-step repair, oracle-targeted repair (upper bound), and uncertainty-targeted repair. Our analysis reveals three findings. First, oracle-targeted repair substantially outperforms full restart, establishing that step-level targeting is worth pursuing. Second, uncertainty-targeted repair closes much of the gap to the oracle, demonstrating that internal model signals are informative for error localization despite imperfect calibration. Third, cascade-aware localization rules — which account for the tendency of errors to propagate downstream, causing uncertainty to peak *after* the true failure point — consistently improve localization accuracy over standard argmax selection. We further introduce error-informed nudges, where the repair prompt incorporates the predicted error type, and show they yield additional gains over generic retry hints. All experiments use controlled comparisons with paired statistical tests, three random seeds, and a validated 72B judge for ground-truth annotation. Code and reproducibility artifacts are publicly available.
